## When to use

Reach for this when you have (or expect) an offer and need to negotiate comp — base, equity, bonus, or terms — from an evidence-backed position instead of a hopeful guess. Use after car-interview-prep, once you have leverage.

**Not for:** the employer side. Setting pay bands, running internal comp benchmarking, or authoring the offer is hr-comp-analysis and hr-draft-offer. This skill arms *you*, the candidate receiving the offer.

## Method

1. **Gather market data** — pull ranges for the exact title, level, and location from multiple sources (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, Blind, peers). Decision point: if data is thin, widen to adjacent titles and note the uncertainty rather than anchoring on one number.
2. **Set your numbers** — define your walk-away (minimum), target (realistic ask), and aspirational (opening) figures across the full package, not just base.
3. **Establish your BATNA** — your best alternative: a competing offer, current job, or runway. Decision point: no BATNA means lead with value and fit, not ultimatums.
4. **Value the whole offer** — base, bonus, equity (with strike/vesting/valuation assumptions), sign-on, benefits, PTO, remote. Normalize competing offers to compare like-for-like.
5. **Script the moves** — the ask, the counter, the "let me think it over," and the graceful accept/decline. Anchor high but defensible with a reason.
6. **Negotiate calmly** — get the offer in writing, express genuine enthusiasm, then negotiate. Ask for time; never accept on the call.

## Example

Offer: $150K base. Market for the level is $155-180K; you have a competing offer at $165K. Script: "I'm genuinely excited about this role and the team. Based on my research for this level and a competing offer I'm holding at $165K, I was targeting $175K base. Is there flexibility there?" Then silence. If base is capped, pivot: "If base is fixed, could we look at the sign-on or additional equity?"

## Pitfalls

- Negotiating on base alone and leaving equity, sign-on, and PTO — often more flexible — on the table.
- Giving a number first without market data, anchoring yourself low.
- Bluffing a BATNA that doesn't exist; a called bluff destroys trust and leverage.
- Accepting on the phone out of relief; always ask for time and get it in writing.

## Output format

```
Role + level + location
Market data: source | range | median  (x2-3 sources)
Your numbers: walk-away | target | opening  (per component)
BATNA: alternative | strength | expiry
Offer valuation: base | bonus | equity(assumptions) | sign-on | benefits | total
Scripts: the ask | counter | "thinking it over" | accept | decline
```
