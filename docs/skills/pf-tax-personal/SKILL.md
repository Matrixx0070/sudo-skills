## When to use

Use this when the owner wants to get organized for tax season — gathering documents, building a checklist, and making sure common deductions and credits are considered before they file or hand off to a preparer.

**Not for:** computing an exact tax liability, filing returns, giving jurisdiction-specific rulings, or advising on aggressive positions. Tax law varies by country/state and changes yearly.

This skill provides education and organization, not individualized tax advice. Recommend a licensed tax professional (CPA/EA or local equivalent) before filing anything with real stakes.

## Method

1. Identify the owner's situation: employment type (W-2/employed, self-employed, both), filing status, and jurisdiction. Note that specifics vary by location.
2. Build the income-document checklist (wage statements, contractor income, interest/dividends, sale-of-asset records).
3. Decision point — standard deduction vs itemizing: itemizing only helps if total itemized deductions exceed the standard deduction. Sum the candidates first.
4. Surface commonly missed items to *ask the preparer about*: retirement contributions, HSA, student-loan interest, charitable gifts, home-office/self-employed expenses, education credits, childcare/dependent credits.
5. Organize records into a folder structure by category; note anything missing.
6. Decision point — any life change this year (marriage, child, home purchase, new self-employment)? Flag it; these shift eligibility significantly.
7. Note key dates: filing deadline and any estimated-payment dates for self-employed income.

## Example

Owner: employed + freelance side income, single. Standard deduction $14,600; itemized candidates total $9,200 → take the standard. Flags to raise with preparer: IRA contribution room, home-office deduction on freelance income, and $600 charitable gifts (won't help unless itemizing). New this year: started freelancing → estimated-tax obligation flagged. Documents missing: one 1099 not yet received.

## Pitfalls

- Itemizing out of habit when the standard deduction is larger — do the comparison every year.
- Losing receipts for deductible expenses; without records the deduction is not defensible.
- Missing quarterly estimated payments on self-employment income, triggering penalties.
- Treating this checklist as a filed return. It organizes; a professional confirms and files.
