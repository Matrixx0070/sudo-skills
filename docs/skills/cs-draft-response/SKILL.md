When to use: you need to reply to a customer — answering a question, delivering bad news, apologizing for an outage, declining a request, or following up. Use this to produce a reply that is clear, human, and on-brand.

METHOD
1. Read the customer's message for the real ask and their emotional state (frustrated, confused, urgent, calm). Match tone to state: lead with acknowledgment when they are upset, lead with the answer when they are neutral.
2. Decide the outcome you can deliver: full fix, partial fix + timeline, workaround, or a "no" with a reason and alternative. Never over-promise.
3. Structure the reply: acknowledge → answer/action → next steps → invitation to follow up. Put the most important line first for scanners.
4. Be specific — reference their account, name the exact steps, give real dates. Vague reassurance ("we're looking into it") erodes trust.
5. For bad news or a decline: apologize once and sincerely, explain briefly, then pivot to what you CAN do. Avoid blame, jargon, and defensive language.
6. Close by making the next move easy and set expectations on response time.

OUTPUT FORMAT
- Suggested subject line (if email)
- Tone read: one line on detected sentiment + chosen register
- Draft response (ready to send)
- Optional shorter variant (for chat/quick reply)
- Notes: anything to verify or personalize before sending
