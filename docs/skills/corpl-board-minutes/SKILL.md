## When to use

Use this to prepare minutes for a board or board-committee meeting — regular, special, or a significant transaction approval. Good minutes evidence that directors were informed, deliberated, and acted with due care, which is exactly what a court or acquirer's diligence team later scrutinizes.

**Not for:** action taken without a meeting (use corpl-written-consent); stockholder meeting minutes without adjusting the framework; a verbatim transcript — minutes summarize, they do not transcribe. Any minutes recording a conflicted transaction, a major acquisition, or a fiduciary-sensitive vote go to counsel before finalizing.

## Method

1. **Record the formalities first.** Date, time, place/means (in person or remote), attendees present/absent, who chaired, who kept the record, and confirmation that notice and quorum were satisfied.
2. **List materials distributed** and note that directors had the opportunity to review them.
3. **Summarize each agenda item** as: matter presented → who presented → that the board discussed and asked questions → resolution. Capture the *fact* of deliberation, not every remark.
4. **State resolutions precisely.** Use "RESOLVED, that…" language and record the vote (unanimous, or for/against/abstain with names where required).
5. **Document conflicts and recusals.** *Decision point:* if a director is interested in a matter, record disclosure of the interest and the director's recusal from the vote — omission is a diligence red flag and a fiduciary exposure.
6. **Note follow-ups and adjournment.** Action items with owners; time of adjournment.
7. **Route for review**, then circulate as a draft for approval at the next meeting.

## Example

Special meeting, remote, 6 of 7 directors present (quorum met), CFO presented the acquisition term sheet distributed 48 hours prior. Board discussed valuation and financing, questioned management, and, Director Lee having disclosed an interest in the target and recused, adopted: "RESOLVED, that the Company is authorized to enter the transaction substantially on the presented terms." Vote: unanimous of those voting (Lee recused).

## Pitfalls

- **Transcribing debate.** Detailed dissent narratives can be used against the board later; record process, not blow-by-blow.
- **Omitting quorum/notice confirmation.** Without it, the validity of the action is attackable.
- **Skipping conflict disclosure and recusal.** This is the single most consequential gap in transaction minutes.
- **Finalizing before the next-meeting approval.** Minutes are a draft until formally approved.

## Output format

```
MINUTES — <Board/Committee> of <Entity>
Date / Time / Place or means:
Present / Absent / Chair / Secretary:
Notice & quorum: confirmed
Materials distributed:
Agenda items:
  <matter> — presented by <name> — board discussed & questioned — RESOLVED: <text> — Vote: <result>
Conflicts / recusals:
Action items (owner):
Adjournment:
Status: DRAFT — attorney review: <name> — for approval at next meeting
```

## Reference

Minute-keeping formalities: confirm notice and quorum, record deliberation as process, state resolutions in operative language, and document interested-director disclosure and recusal. Retain minutes in the corporate minute book with signed/approved versions. Sensitive matters (litigation, M&A, conflicts) are drafted "Privileged & Confidential" and reviewed by counsel before entry.
