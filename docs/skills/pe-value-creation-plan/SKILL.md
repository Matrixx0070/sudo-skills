## When to use

Use this when a deal is signed or closed and you need to turn the investment thesis into an owned, sequenced execution plan — what gets done in the first 100 days, what happens across the full hold, who owns each initiative, and how each lever maps to the EBITDA, margin, and de-risking gains the returns model assumes. Reach for it in the pre-close planning window, at close to launch the 100-day sprint, or at a reset when the plan drifts from the thesis. The output is a value-creation plan (VCP): a lever map, a 100-day plan, a full-hold roadmap, and a KPI/owner/milestone structure tied back to the returns bridge.

**Not for:** assessing AI maturity and scoping AI initiatives specifically (use pe-ai-readiness — feed its output in as the digital/AI lever), running pre-signing diligence (use pe-dd-checklist), preparing diligence-meeting questions (use pe-dd-meeting-prep), the go/no-go screen (use pe-deal-screening), building the deal pipeline (use pe-deal-sourcing), writing the committee memo (use pe-ic-memo), tracking the plan's KPIs and covenants after launch (use pe-portfolio-monitoring — it monitors what this skill defines), quantifying the MOIC/IRR the plan must deliver (use pe-returns-analysis — the VCP is its operational counterpart), or dissecting unit economics that underlie the commercial levers (use pe-unit-economics).

## Method

1. Anchor to the thesis and the bridge. Restate the returns thesis and the value-creation bridge from the returns model: how much EBITDA growth, margin, and de-risking the deal underwrites. Every lever in the plan must trace to a number the model needs. **Decision:** if the underwritten EBITDA gain cannot be attributed to concrete, owned levers, the plan is not yet fundable — go back to the levers, not the model.
2. Inventory the levers. Work through the standard set — commercial/revenue growth, pricing, margin/cost-out, working-capital release, M&A/buy-and-build, digital/AI, and multiple expansion via de-risking — and size the EBITDA or cash impact and confidence of each for this company.
3. Separate quick wins from structural plays. **Decision:** classify each lever as a quick win (cash or EBITDA in <6 months, low dependency — e.g. pricing, procurement, DSO) versus structural (12–36 months, capability-building — e.g. new segment, ERP, buy-and-build). Load the 100-day plan with quick wins and thesis-critical setup; sequence structural plays across the hold.
4. Build the 100-day plan. Define the first 100 days: management assessment and any leadership changes, the reporting/KPI baseline and data infrastructure, quick-win initiatives launched, the value-creation team and governance (board cadence, operating partners), and the confirmed full-hold roadmap. **Decision:** decide the management verdict early — back, augment, or replace the CEO/CFO — because most of the plan depends on the team that executes it.
5. Build the full-hold roadmap. Phase the structural levers across years 1–3+ (and to exit): sequencing, capital required, dependencies, and the value-creation milestones that de-risk the business for a premium exit.
6. Assign owners, milestones, and KPIs. For every initiative name a single accountable owner (management, not the fund), a target date, the capital/investment needed, and 1–3 measurable KPIs with baselines and targets. Unowned initiatives do not happen.
7. Wire to monitoring and the exit. Define how each KPI rolls into board reporting (handoff to portfolio monitoring), and describe the exit narrative the plan builds toward — the de-risked, higher-quality business a buyer pays a premium for.

## Example

A sponsor buys a $50m-EBITDA field-services business underwriting EBITDA growth to $80m over five years with a flat exit multiple. The VCP attributes the $30m gain to concrete levers: pricing (a 3% list increase plus discount-discipline, +$6m, quick win), procurement and route-density cost-out (+$5m, quick win + structural), a new adjacent service line (+$7m, structural, year 1–3), and a buy-and-build of three regional bolt-ons (+$12m combined, structural, years 2–4, each acquired at ~6x and re-rated into the platform's ~10x). The 100-day plan replaces the CFO, stands up a monthly KPI pack and a data warehouse, launches the pricing initiative and a procurement RFP, and confirms the M&A pipeline; the CEO is backed with an incentive reset. Each lever carries an owner (COO owns cost-out, a new VP owns the service line, the CEO owns M&A), a milestone date, and KPIs (price realization, gross margin, DSO, pipeline coverage) that feed straight into the portfolio-monitoring pack. Multiple expansion is deliberately not underwritten — the de-risking (recurring revenue mix, systems, management depth) is framed as exit-narrative upside, not modeled return.

## Pitfalls

- **A plan not tied to the bridge.** A list of good ideas that does not sum to the underwritten EBITDA gain leaves the return unfunded. Every lever must map to a number the returns model depends on, and the levers must reconcile to the bridge.
- **Owned by the fund, not management.** Initiatives owned by "the deal team" or "operating partners" without a named accountable executive stall. The management team executes; the fund governs. Assign one accountable owner per initiative and fix the management verdict early.
- **All structural, no quick wins.** A plan that delivers nothing for 18 months burns credibility and cash. Front-load pricing, procurement, and working-capital wins to fund and de-risk the structural plays.
- **Underwriting multiple expansion as a lever.** De-risking earns a better exit multiple, but treating multiple expansion as a planned deliverable turns an operating plan into a market bet. Frame de-risking as building exit-narrative quality, and keep modeled return in EBITDA and cash.
- **KPIs without baselines.** A target with no measured starting point cannot show progress or variance. Baseline every KPI in the 100 days and define exactly how it rolls into board reporting.

## Reference

Substantive overview of value-creation planning in PE. Impacts are illustrative and must be sized from diligence and the operating model; ownership and timelines are set with the management team.

### Why the VCP exists

As entry multiples have risen and cheap leverage has receded, returns increasingly come from operational value creation rather than financial engineering or market beta. The VCP is the bridge from the investment thesis to execution: it translates "we underwrote EBITDA growth of $30m" into named initiatives, owners, and KPIs, and it is the document the operating-partner model and board govern against for the full hold.

### The value-creation levers

| Lever | What it does | Typical horizon | Notes |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|-------|
| Commercial / revenue growth | New customers, segments, geographies, cross-sell, sales-force effectiveness | Structural (1–3 yr) | Largest EBITDA-growth source; needs unit-economics grounding |
| Pricing | List increases, discount discipline, packaging, value-based pricing | Quick win | Highest-ROI lever; flows almost entirely to EBITDA |
| Margin / cost-out | Procurement, footprint, org design, automation, COGS | Quick win + structural | Fast wins in procurement; structural in footprint/systems |
| Working capital | DSO, DPO, inventory release | Quick win | Frees cash for debt paydown / bolt-ons; improves FCF leg of bridge |
| M&A / buy-and-build | Bolt-on acquisitions rolled into the platform | Structural (2–4 yr) | Multiple arbitrage: buy small at low multiple, re-rate into platform |
| Digital / AI | Automation, data products, analytics-driven ops (see pe-ai-readiness) | Structural | Enables both revenue and cost levers |
| Multiple expansion via de-risking | Scale, recurring revenue, management depth, systems → premium exit | Full hold | Earned, not planned as modeled return |

### Quick wins vs structural initiatives

| | Quick wins | Structural plays |
|--|-----------|------------------|
| Horizon | <6 months | 12–36 months |
| Examples | Pricing, procurement, DSO, discount discipline | New segment, ERP/systems, buy-and-build, footprint |
| Dependency | Low; existing team | High; capability and capital build |
| Role in plan | Fund and de-risk the hold, build credibility | Deliver the bulk of underwritten EBITDA growth |

Sequence quick wins into the 100 days both for the EBITDA/cash they generate and because early proof points earn the management team room to run the structural plays.

### The 100-day plan

The first 100 days set the trajectory for the whole hold. Standard workstreams:

| Workstream | Objective |
|-----------|-----------|
| Management assessment | Verdict on CEO/CFO/key roles — back, augment, or replace — and incentive/equity alignment |
| Reporting & data baseline | Stand up the KPI pack, data infrastructure, and single source of truth |
| Quick-win launch | Kick off pricing, procurement, and working-capital initiatives |
| Governance | Board composition and cadence, operating-partner engagement, VCP ownership |
| Roadmap confirmation | Validate and lock the full-hold plan and M&A pipeline |

The management verdict is the pivotal decision: the plan is executed by the team, so getting the right operators in seat early is often the single highest-value action in the 100 days.

### Full-hold roadmap and linkage to the returns bridge

The full-hold roadmap phases structural levers across the ownership period and ties each to the returns bridge:

- EBITDA-growth line ← commercial, pricing, margin/cost-out, buy-and-build (organic + acquired EBITDA)
- FCF / deleveraging line ← working-capital release and margin improvement feeding debt paydown
- Exit-multiple line ← de-risking milestones (recurring revenue mix, customer concentration reduction, systems, management depth) that support a premium or at-least-defensible exit multiple

Every initiative should reconcile to one of these lines; the sum of lever impacts should equal the bridge the returns model underwrote. A VCP that does not reconcile to the bridge signals either an over-optimistic model or an under-specified plan.

### Owners, milestones, KPIs

Discipline that makes a VCP execute rather than decorate:

- One accountable owner per initiative — a named executive, not "the fund" or a committee.
- A milestone date and the capital/investment required for each.
- 1–3 KPIs per initiative, each with a measured baseline and a target, that roll into the monthly/quarterly board pack (handoff to pe-portfolio-monitoring).
- A cadence for review — the board and operating partners track variance against the VCP, not just the budget.
